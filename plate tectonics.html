Plate Tectonics

Types of Volcanoes

  • Strato-volcanoes (Composite Cone Volcanoes):
    • Structure: Tall, steep-sided, and conical, formed by alternating layers of lava, ash, and volcanic debris.
    • Characteristics: Explosive eruptions due to viscous magma with high silica content, commonly found near subduction zones.
    • Examples: Mount Fuji (Japan), Mount St. Helens (USA).
  • Shield Volcanoes:
    • Structure: Broad and gently sloping, formed by the accumulation of low-viscosity basaltic lava.
    • Characteristics: Non-explosive eruptions with lava flowing over long distances, common at divergent plate boundaries and hotspots.
    • Examples: Mauna Loa and Kilauea (Hawaii).

Features of Volcanoes

  • Crater: A bowl-shaped depression at the top of a volcano, formed by explosive activity or collapse.
  • Vent: The opening through which magma, gases, and volcanic material are ejected.
  • Magma Chamber: A reservoir of molten rock beneath the Earth's surface that feeds eruptions.

Features of Earthquakes

  • Epicentre: The point on the Earth's surface directly above the earthquake's origin.
  • Focus (Hypocentre): The location beneath the Earth's surface where the earthquake begins.
  • Magnitude: A measure of the energy released during an earthquake, often measured on the Richter scale.

Global Pattern of Plates and Their Structure

  • The Earth's lithosphere is divided into tectonic plates that float on the semi-fluid asthenosphere.
  • Plate Movements:
    • Constructive/Divergent Boundaries: Plates move apart, forming new crust and mid-ocean ridges (e.g., Mid-Atlantic Ridge).
    • Destructive/Convergent Boundaries: Plates move toward each other, leading to subduction, volcanic activity, and mountain formation (e.g., Andes Mountains).
    • Conservative/Transform Boundaries: Plates slide past one another, causing earthquakes (e.g., San Andreas Fault).
  • Subduction Zones: Areas where one plate is forced beneath another, creating trenches, volcanic arcs, and seismic activity.

Effects of Plate Movements

  • Constructive Boundaries: Formation of new crust, mid-ocean ridges, and shield volcanoes.
  • Destructive Boundaries: Formation of trenches, composite volcanoes, earthquakes, and mountain ranges.
  • Conservative Boundaries: Frequent earthquakes due to the friction of sliding plates.

Comparison of Earthquake Effects in MEDCs and LEDCs

Comparison of Effects

Aspect MEDCs LEDCs
Infrastructure Damage Advanced infrastructure built to withstand earthquakes often limits damage. However, older structures may still collapse. Poorly constructed buildings and infrastructure are highly vulnerable, leading to widespread destruction.
Loss of Life Fewer casualties due to better emergency response systems, earthquake-resistant designs, and advanced technology. High death tolls due to poorly built homes, lack of emergency plans, and slower rescue efforts.
Economic Impact Higher monetary losses due to expensive infrastructure, but the economy usually recovers faster. Severe economic setbacks, as recovery is slower and resources for rebuilding are limited.
Healthcare Systems Well-equipped healthcare systems can handle injuries more effectively, reducing fatalities. Overburdened or underfunded healthcare systems struggle to provide adequate care.
Emergency Response Quick and efficient emergency services minimize loss of life and damage. Limited resources and delayed responses exacerbate the situation.

Comparison of How MEDCs and LEDCs Cope with Earthquakes

How They Cope

Category Strategies
MEDCs
  • Invest in earthquake-resistant infrastructure, using advanced materials and designs.
  • Conduct regular earthquake drills and educate the public on safety measures.
  • Have robust emergency response systems with well-trained personnel and advanced technology.
  • Use insurance to cover reconstruction costs and mitigate economic losses.
LEDCs
  • Often rely on international aid and support for disaster relief and rebuilding efforts.
  • Implement community-based disaster management programs to raise awareness and preparedness.
  • Gradually adopt earthquake-resistant building techniques, though progress is slow due to limited resources.
  • Focus on strengthening early warning systems and evacuation plans as funding allows.

Benefits of Living Near Volcanoes and Hazardous Areas

Some Benefits of Living Near Volcanoes

  • Fertile Soil: Volcanic ash enriches soil with nutrients, making it ideal for agriculture.
  • Geothermal Energy: Volcanic areas often provide a sustainable source of geothermal energy.
  • Tourism Opportunities: Volcanoes attract tourists, boosting the local economy.
  • Mineral Resources: Volcanoes can produce valuable minerals like sulfur, gold, and diamonds.
  • Cultural and Historical Significance: Many volcanic regions hold cultural and religious importance for local communities.

Some Other Reasons People Live in Hazardous Areas

  • Economic Opportunities: Fertile land, mining, or tourism can make hazardous areas attractive.
  • Family and Tradition: People may have deep cultural or ancestral ties to the area.
  • Lack of Alternatives: Limited resources may prevent relocation to safer areas.
  • Ignorance of Risks: People may underestimate the risks or believe they won't be affected.
  • Infrastructure: Developed infrastructure in hazardous areas often outweighs the risk for many.

Case Studies

Case Study 1: MEDC - Mount St. Helens, USA (1980 Eruption)

  • Impact: The eruption destroyed 250 homes, 47 bridges, and caused massive damage to forestry and infrastructure. 57 people died.
  • Response: The USA's advanced emergency response systems evacuated many people before the eruption, limiting the death toll.
  • Rebuilding: The government invested heavily in rebuilding infrastructure, restoring the affected areas, and studying volcanic behavior.
  • Preparedness: Long-term monitoring systems were established to provide better warnings of future eruptions.

Case Study 2: LEDC - Mount Nyiragongo, Democratic Republic of Congo (2002 Eruption)

  • Impact: The eruption killed 245 people, left 120,000 homeless, and caused extensive damage to the city of Goma.
  • Response: Limited emergency services struggled to respond effectively. International aid provided food, shelter, and medical support.
  • Challenges: Poor infrastructure and lack of resources slowed recovery efforts. Many people were forced to return to dangerous areas.
  • Lessons Learned: Highlighted the need for better disaster management and international support in LEDCs.
